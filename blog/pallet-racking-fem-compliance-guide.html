FEM 10.2.02 Racking Compliance: What European Buyers Must Know

The definitive guide to European pallet racking standards, CE marking, and how to verify supplier compliance

← Back to Blog
Published: May 9, 2026 | Reading Time: 10 minutes | Author: RackingHub Team

If you're specifying pallet racking for a warehouse in the EU or UK, FEM 10.2.02 isn't optional — it's the engineering baseline that inspectors, insurers, and building authorities expect to see referenced in your design documentation. Non-compliant racking can mean denied insurance claims, failed building inspections, or worse, a structural failure under load. This guide breaks down exactly what FEM 10.2.02 requires, how it relates to EN 15512, and what you should verify with your supplier before placing an order.

What Is FEM 10.2.02 and FEM 10.2.09?

FEM stands for the European Federation of Materials Handling (Fédération Européenne de la Manutention), the industry body that publishes technical rules for storage equipment across Europe.

Two rules matter most for pallet racking:

  • FEM 10.2.02 — Steel static storage systems: adjustable pallet racking. This covers the structural design principles, including load assumptions, load combinations, and safety factors for the racking frame itself.
  • FEM 10.2.09 — Recommendations for the design of static steel pallet racking. This is a companion document that provides practical guidance on applying FEM 10.2.02, including specific guidance on upright frame design, bracing systems, and connection details.

These rules were developed by an industry consortium — racking manufacturers, testing labs, and structural engineers — to create a unified approach to racking design across Europe. Before FEM, each country had its own interpretation, making cross-border procurement a headache. The FEM rules solved that.

FEM 10.2.02 vs. EN 15512: How They Relate

This is where buyers get confused, so let's clarify:

Standard Type Scope
FEM 10.2.02 Industry technical rule Structural design of adjustable pallet racking
EN 15512 European Standard (CEN) Design of steel static storage systems — broader scope, more prescriptive
EN 15635 European Standard (CEN) Safe use and maintenance of storage equipment

EN 15512 was largely derived from FEM 10.2.02 but went through the formal CEN standardization process. The core design methodology is the same — both use limit state design with partial safety factors. EN 15512 is more formal, more detailed, and carries greater legal weight because it's a harmonized European standard referenced in the Machinery Directive.

The bottom line: If a racking system is designed to FEM 10.2.02, it is substantially aligned with EN 15512. In practice, reputable European manufacturers design to both simultaneously. The key is asking for documented proof — calculation reports and test certificates — not just a claim on a spec sheet.

Core Requirements: The Numbers You Need to Know

FEM 10.2.02 specifies several critical design parameters. Here's what matters when you're evaluating a supplier:

Design Loads and Load Combinations

The standard requires designers to consider multiple load cases simultaneously:

  • Dead load: Self-weight of the racking structure
  • Imposed load: Weight of stored goods (the pallet loads you specify)
  • Accidental loads: Forklift impact forces — minimum 10 kN at 0.5m height for unprotected uprights at aisle ends
  • Wind and seismic loads: Where applicable (seismic is mandatory in zones like southern Europe; wind matters for outdoor or partially enclosed installations)

These loads are combined using partial factors. For example, the ultimate limit state (ULS) combination typically applies 1.35 to permanent loads and 1.50 to variable loads. Your supplier should be able to show you which load combinations they've used in their calculations.

Safety Factors

FEM 10.2.02 uses partial safety factors (γ) on both loads and material resistance:

  • Material resistance factor (γM): Typically 1.0 to 1.1 for steel members
  • Load factors: 1.35 for permanent loads, 1.50 for variable loads
  • Overall safety factor on load capacity: Falls in the range of 1.5 to 2.0, depending on the specific member and failure mode

The variation between 1.5 and 2.0 isn't arbitrary — it depends on whether the member's behavior is ductile or brittle, and on the confidence level in the test data for that specific cross-section profile. Cold-formed sections (common in racking uprights) typically require higher factors because their behavior under buckling is more complex than hot-rolled steel.

Deflection Limits

Deflection isn't just about aesthetics — excessive deflection compromises pallet stability and forklift operation. FEM 10.2.02 sets these limits:

  • Horizontal deflection at top of upright: Maximum H/200, where H is the total rack height. For a 10-meter rack, that's 50mm maximum sway.
  • Beam deflection under full load: Maximum L/200, where L is the beam span. For a 2.7-meter beam, that's 13.5mm maximum.
  • Frame sway between levels: Maximum h/300 per level, where h is the storey height.

Engineering Note: The H/200 limit on upright deflection is the one most often violated by under-designed systems. If a supplier can't provide a deflection calculation for your specific configuration, that's a red flag.

Why FEM Compliance Actually Matters

Legal and Regulatory

In the EU, racking systems fall under the scope of the Construction Products Regulation (CPR) and, depending on the configuration, may also be considered machinery. EN 15512 (derived from FEM 10.2.02) is the referenced design standard. Non-compliant racking can fail building inspections and may be illegal to install in commercial warehouses.

Insurance

Most commercial property insurance policies require that storage equipment meets applicable European standards. If a racking collapse occurs and your insurer finds the system was not designed to FEM 10.2.02 / EN 15512, they can — and often do — deny the claim entirely. We've seen cases where the denial covered millions in product damage.

Safety and Liability

Beyond compliance paperwork, non-compliant racking fails under real-world conditions. Common failure modes include:

  • Upright buckling under loads that exceed the true (not rated) capacity
  • Progressive collapse triggered by a single forklift impact on an unprotected upright
  • Beam failure due to inadequate connection design
  • Excessive sway causing pallets to shift and fall

How Boracs Ensures FEM Compliance

At Boracs (our manufacturing arm), FEM compliance isn't an afterthought — it's built into the engineering process from day one:

1. Structural Calculations

Every racking configuration we supply comes with full structural calculation reports based on FEM 10.2.02 and EN 15512. These include:

  • Load combination analysis for all relevant load cases
  • Member capacity checks (uprights, beams, braces)
  • Deflection verification against H/200 and L/200 limits
  • Base plate and anchor bolt design calculations

These reports are available for review before you place an order — not after.

2. Physical Testing

Calculations alone aren't enough. We validate our designs through physical testing:

  • Upright compression tests: Verify buckling capacity of cold-formed sections
  • Beam-to-upright connection tests: Confirm moment resistance and rotational stiffness of beam end connectors
  • Full-scale frame tests: Validate overall structural behavior under realistic loading

Test reports are available for key section profiles. If your project requires testing of a specific configuration, we can arrange it.

3. CE Marking Documentation

All racking systems we supply for the European market come with a Declaration of Performance (DoP) and CE marking documentation. This includes:

  • Declaration of Performance referencing EN 15512
  • Technical file with design calculations
  • Material certificates for steel (yield strength, tensile strength)
  • Welding procedure specifications where applicable

4. Quality Control

Manufacturing quality is where theoretical compliance meets reality. We control:

  • Steel material traceability (heat numbers, mill certificates)
  • Dimensional tolerances on roll-formed profiles
  • Welding quality (visual inspection + NDT where required)
  • Powder coating thickness and adhesion testing

Common Non-Compliance Issues (and What They Cost)

We see the same problems repeatedly when buyers source racking without due diligence:

Issue Consequence
No calculation report provided You have no proof the racking can safely support your loads. If a failure occurs, liability falls entirely on you.
Steel thickness understated Supplier claims 2.0mm uprights but delivers 1.5mm. Load capacity drops 20-30%. This is a common cost-cutting measure.
Incorrect load capacity labels Labels show 2,000 kg per level when the actual calculated capacity is 1,400 kg. Operators load to the label. The rack fails.
No impact protection at aisle ends Uprights at aisle ends are vulnerable to forklift strikes. Without column guards rated to 10 kN, a single impact can compromise the frame.
Missing beam safety locks Beams can dislodge from uprights under uplift forces (e.g., forklift lifting a stuck pallet). Missing locks = collapse risk.

Real Case: A distributor in Germany installed racking from an unverified Asian supplier. During a routine EN 15635 inspection, an independent engineer found the upright wall thickness was 0.4mm below spec and no calculation report existed. The entire installation — 400+ pallet positions — had to be replaced. Total cost: €180,000, plus three weeks of warehouse downtime.

Supplier Checklist: What to Ask Before Ordering

Before you sign a purchase order for pallet racking destined for a European warehouse, ask your supplier these questions and get written answers:

  1. "Can you provide FEM 10.2.02 / EN 15512 calculation reports for my specific configuration?" — If they can't or won't, stop here.
  2. "What safety factor is applied to the rated load capacity?" — Should be clearly stated, typically 1.5 to 2.0.
  3. "Can you provide physical test reports for your upright profiles and beam connections?" — Third-party testing is preferable.
  4. "Do you provide a Declaration of Performance and CE marking documentation?" — Required for EU installations.
  5. "What steel grade and thickness are used for uprights and beams?" — Verify against the calculation report. Common grades: S235, S275, S355.
  6. "Are load capacity labels included with the installation?" — EN 15635 requires visible load notices.
  7. "Is the installation design including impact protection for aisle-end uprights?" — Column guards should be standard at aisle ends.
  8. "Can you provide installation drawings and a component list?" — See our warehouse racking installation guide for what proper documentation looks like.

If a supplier hesitates on any of these, that's useful information. A manufacturer that designs to FEM standards will have all of this readily available.

Drive-In Racking and FEM Compliance

If you're considering drive-in racking for high-density storage, FEM compliance becomes even more critical. Drive-in systems have different structural characteristics — fewer aisles means each upright carries more load, and forklifts operating inside the rack structure increase impact risk. The design calculations must account for these additional factors, and inspection frequency should be higher than for selective racking.

Final Thoughts

FEM 10.2.02 compliance isn't just about checking a regulatory box. It's about ensuring that the racking system you install will safely carry the loads you put on it — today, next year, and ten years from now. The cost of compliance is built into well-engineered racking from the start. The cost of non-compliance shows up when something fails.

When sourcing pallet racking for a European warehouse, ask for the calculation reports, verify the steel specs, check the test certificates, and confirm the CE marking documentation. If you're buying from a supplier that can't provide all of this, the price you're saving upfront will be dwarfed by the risk you're accepting.
