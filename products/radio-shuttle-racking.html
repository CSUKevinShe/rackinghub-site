Radio Shuttle Racking Systems for High-Density Storage

Semi-automated FIFO/LIFO pallet storage — up to 80% more capacity than selective racking

Request Quote WhatsApp

Radio shuttle racking is a semi-automated high-density storage system that uses a battery-powered, radio-controlled shuttle cart to transport pallets within deep storage lanes. The operator places the shuttle on the entry rail via forklift, then remotely directs it to position or retrieve pallets at designated depths. This design eliminates the need for forklifts to enter the rack structure, reducing aisle requirements by up to 60%. Each pallet position supports 500-1500 kg, and the system operates in FIFO or LIFO mode. The frame is manufactured from cold-rolled steel Q235B, powder-coated for corrosion resistance, and engineered to FEM 10.2.02 and FEM 10.2.09 standards.

FIFO vs LIFO — Which Mode to Choose

FIFO (First-In, First-Out): Loading occurs at one end of the lane, retrieval at the opposite end. Pallets move in a single direction, ensuring the oldest stock ships first. Requires access aisles on both sides of the rack. Standard choice for perishable goods, food products, and pharmaceuticals with batch expiry dates.

LIFO (Last-In, First-Out): Both loading and retrieval use the same aisle. The most recently placed pallet is removed first. LIFO achieves higher storage density because it needs only one access aisle per block. Best suited for non-perishable products, building materials, and goods where batch sequencing is not critical.

Applications

  • Cold storage warehouses: Reduces cooled aisle count, cutting refrigeration energy by 30-50%. Shuttle rated for -30°C operation.
  • Food and beverage distribution: FIFO lanes manage batch codes and expiry dates. High throughput at entry/exit supports fast-moving consumer goods.
  • High-volume, low-SKU operations: Facilities storing 5-50 SKUs in large quantities — dairy, bottled water, paper — benefit from deep-lane configurations where each lane holds a single product line.
  • Port and logistics buffer storage: Lanes serve as staging for import/export cargo, handling irregular inbound volumes without requiring additional floor space.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a radio shuttle racking system?

A semi-automated high-density pallet storage solution using a battery-powered shuttle cart, controlled via radio remote, to place and retrieve pallets within deep storage lanes. The operator positions the shuttle on the entry rail with a forklift, then directs it to carry pallets to specific positions. This eliminates forklift entry into the rack, increasing storage density by up to 80% vs. selective racking.

What is the difference between FIFO and LIFO in shuttle racking?

FIFO loads pallets from one aisle and retrieves them from the opposite end, ensuring oldest stock ships first — essential for perishable goods and products with batch expiry dates. LIFO uses a single aisle for both loading and retrieval, maximizing lane depth and minimizing floor space, suited for non-perishable items where batch rotation is not critical.

How much weight can a radio shuttle racking system hold?

Standard configurations support 500-1500 kg per pallet position. Capacity depends on upright gauge (1.5-3.0 mm), beam section (1.5-2.5 mm), lane depth, and pallet dimensions. All designs are calculated per FEM 10.2.02 standards. Heavier loads require engineered solutions with thicker materials.

Can a radio shuttle racking system be used in cold storage?

Yes. The shuttle cart and rack structure are rated for environments down to -30°C. Because only one access aisle requires cooling, refrigeration energy drops 30-50%. The powder-coated Q235B steel resists corrosion in humid, low-temperature conditions.

How is a radio shuttle racking system installed and maintained?

Installation follows FEM 10.2.02: site survey, anchor bolt placement, upright assembly, beam locking, and shuttle calibration. Boracs provides installation drawings and remote guidance. Routine maintenance includes monthly battery checks, drive wheel inspection, and annual structural verification per FEM 10.2.09. The lithium-ion battery lasts 8-10 hours per charge.
